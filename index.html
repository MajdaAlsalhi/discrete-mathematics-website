C OURSE DESCRIPTION A course in Discrete Mathematics is designed to prepare IT majors for a background in abstraction, notation, and critical thinking for the mathematics most directly related to Information technology. Topics include: Congruences, Counting Techniques, Discrete Probability Theory, Recursion, Generating Functions, Graph Theory & Trees.
Course Aims/Objectives:
 The students must be able to:
1. Extend the basic mathematical awareness and skills.
2. Understand the mathematical background necessary for other compulsory modules.
3. Discover the study skills necessary for students to learn new areas of mathematics
(including those we do not cover in the degree). 4. Attain a range of useful problem solving skills.
5. Adapt the mathematical approach to the study of Information Technology.
Learning Outcomes:
On successful completion of this the students will be able to:
1. Manipulate formulae involving Congruences, and Counting Techniques. 2. Solve simple problems involving Probability Theory, Recursion & its Application, and Generating Functions. 3. Able to apply the concepts of Recursions, Graph Theory and Trees in to Information Technology. 4. Appreciate the importance of discrete mathematics to the understanding of computation. Full Binary Trees
In a full binary tree, every node has either two children or no children. In other words if a node is not a leaf (i.e. if it has at least one child) then both the left and right subtrees of that node are not empty. A full binary tree always has exactly one more leaf than it has internal nodes. This is a great opportunity for an inductive proof. First let’s consider the simplest case, the binary tree: Since L and R are simpler than the original tree, the result can be assumed to hold for them. That is, in each of the two triangles there is one more leaf than internal nodes – two extra leaves among the two triangles. But that is offset by the additional internal node which is the root. So, the entire tree has one more leaf than internal nodes. Traversal When we have a tree, in particular a binary tree, then we frequently want to “visit” each vertex of the tree.
